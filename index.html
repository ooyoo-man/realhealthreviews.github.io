Magnesium Glycinate Review

A comprehensive look at the evidence for magnesium glycinate supplementation: who benefits, how much to take, and what the research actually shows.

What is magnesium glycinate?

Magnesium glycinate is a chelated form of magnesium — meaning the mineral is bonded to the amino acid glycine. This bonding improves absorption in the gut and reduces the laxative effect associated with cheaper forms like magnesium oxide or sulfate.

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body, including protein synthesis, muscle and nerve function, blood glucose control, and blood pressure regulation. Despite its importance, studies suggest that a significant portion of adults in Western countries don't meet the recommended daily intake through diet alone.

Key takeaway: If you're considering magnesium supplementation, glycinate is one of the better-absorbed forms available and is worth the slightly higher cost over oxide.

What does the evidence say?

The strongest evidence for magnesium glycinate centers on three areas: sleep quality, muscle function, and mood. Multiple randomized controlled trials have found that magnesium supplementation in deficient populations improves subjective sleep quality, reduces time to fall asleep, and decreases nighttime waking.

For muscle cramps and recovery, the evidence is good — particularly for athletes and pregnant women, two populations frequently low in magnesium. Several meta-analyses support its use for exercise-related muscle cramps, though the evidence for general nocturnal leg cramps is mixed.

Who benefits most?

People most likely to benefit are those with low dietary intake (common in diets high in processed foods), older adults whose absorption decreases with age, people with type 2 diabetes, and those who consume alcohol regularly. Athletes in heavy training may also benefit due to increased magnesium losses through sweat.

If your magnesium status is already adequate, benefits are likely to be modest. This is a consistent finding across many micronutrient studies — supplementation corrects a deficit; it doesn't provide a supercharge above normal levels.

Dosage and safety

Typical effective doses range from 200–400mg of elemental magnesium per day. Magnesium glycinate capsules often contain 100–200mg of elemental magnesium per dose. The tolerable upper intake level set by health authorities is 350mg/day from supplements (above dietary intake), so stay within this range unless directed by a clinician.

Side effects are rare at standard doses. Very high doses can cause loose stools or diarrhea, though this is far less common with glycinate than oxide forms. Consult a doctor if you take medications including diuretics, antibiotics, or bisphosphonates.
